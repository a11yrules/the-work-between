Soren Hamby is an artist, poet, and advocate. We spend a lot of this conversation talking about poetry because, for Soren, it connects to almost everything else they do.

Soren describes their creative life as having two sides. Knitting and crochet are mostly practical. Poetry and painting are where they express themselves. They talk about a high school English assignment that sparked a love of writing, and how that eventually became a lifeline after moving across the country. A local writing community gave them a way to meet people, but also the confidence to start telling their own story.

We also talk about why specific stories connect with people better than general ones, why editing can create enough distance to write about difficult experiences, and why the value of writing isn’t always in finishing a piece.

Disability is woven throughout the conversation. Soren talks about vision loss, a later-in-life neurodivergence diagnosis, and pushes back against the idea that disabled lives need to be “fixed.” Disability, they say, is neutral. Some parts of it are difficult. Some aren’t. Like anyone else’s life, it’s simply part of who they are.

It’s a conversation about poetry. But it’s also about identity, creativity, disability, and finding your own voice.

### In this episode

- Necessity versus expression: how Soren splits their creative practice between fiber arts made from need and poetry, painting, and drawing made from desire
- Getting back into poetry through a local writing community after a cross-country move
- "Specific is the best general": why precise, personal detail is what makes writing relatable
- Writing for themselves first, and what National Poetry Writing Month is really for
- Editing as distance: dating drafts, revising for years, and why a poem is never really finished
- Vision loss, the pull between physical paper and digital tools like Procreate, and knowing when you've gone too digital
- Writing groups as a controlled, accessible way to build community as a neurodivergent person
- The poem about platitudes, and the conversation it sparked with someone who disagreed
- Late diagnosis of neurodivergence, and the particular difficulty of fighting stereotypes that are harder to see than physical disability
- Disability as a neutral thing, like water, and the frustration of being told a disabled life isn't worth living
- Writing as advocacy, as therapy, and as an alternate dimension for saying what can't be rendered any other way

### A quote that stayed with me

> "Disability... is a completely neutral thing, like water. It is just there. It can be good and can shape my life in ways that I never thought it would. It can also... too much of it can be a lot to carry."

### About Soren

Soren Hamby is an artist and advocate who survives under capitalism as an accessibility and product equity designer. Their natural state is to build systems, frameworks, and communities, and to challenge the hidden structures that determine inclusion. They live in New York’s lower Hudson Valley on unceded Ramapough Lenape land, reshaping perceptions of living with multiple disabilities and intersecting identities. When they’re not busy with small goals like changing the world, you’ll find them painting, doing fiber arts, writing poetry, or collecting different ways of making and creating, which they see as essential human needs.
